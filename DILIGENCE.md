# AI Diligence Statement

This course was built with material assistance from large language models, primarily Anthropic's Claude (Opus 4.6 and 4.7). NotebookLM helped me cross-reference the research corpus, and Claude Design handled the reference materials' layout.

The curriculum architecture for Modules 3 and 4 originated in my Master's work (including the decision to teach LLM limitations through tokenization mechanics) and was adapted here for a corporate workforce audience. Modules 1 and 2 were shaped collaboratively with Claude after I collated and read the underlying research, marking the passages worth foregrounding and the citations worth using. The 4D taxonomy, adopted from Anthropic's AI Fluency Framework, is the fixed scaffold throughout. Claude drafted the instructional prose; I revised it manually. I specified the data visualizations and built them with Claude. The interactive scenarios and knowledge checks we co-created. Claude did most of the platform engineering; I reviewed and directed it. I generated the seven reference PDFs from the written content and laid them out using Claude Design.

Two guardrails defined the engagement. Every citation was hand-selected from research I read in full; Claude was permitted to draw from that list but not to introduce new sources or invent supporting evidence. Citations were also intentionally narrow on this first pass, with plans to expand them. Claude drafted prose but did not decide what to teach. The pedagogical sequencing, the choice of which research to foreground, and the framing of every section were mine.

Human review included a full citation verification pass. Every single citation in the course was hand-checked by me against its source. Beyond that, editing focused on what AI is reliably bad at: voice and internal consistency. I made manual passes stripping the kinds of language patterns AI defaults to, with humanizer tools as a backstop. The build journal records ongoing vocabulary alignment work across modules. The biggest one was the P10 verification activity's classification scheme, which I reworked to describe what learners do (Include / Verify / Flag) rather than what content is. Smaller ones included pulling residual "hallucination" framing in line with Module 3's "specificity" language, and correcting a duration claim that had drifted out of date. No outright fabrications survived.

The course is built for the chatbot interfaces most working professionals interact with daily. Agentic systems, IDE-integrated coding workflows, and fine-tuned domain models are out of scope; they may become later courses. It assumes a college- or university-educated working-adult audience. It is platform-agnostic by design, anchored to structural properties of large language models (next-token prediction, context windows, training-derived knowledge, steerability, tokenization) that won't change much soon, rather than to current-model benchmarks that will. I stand behind the entirety of it. Errors, framing choices, and pedagogical judgments are mine.
