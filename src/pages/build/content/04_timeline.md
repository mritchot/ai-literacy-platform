Two timelines for the same deliverable set. The first is factual — the retrospective record of a solo, AI-accelerated build, reconstructed from a dated process journal. The second estimates how a five-person L&D team would schedule the same scope with conventional methods. The comparison is not an argument for replacing teams with AI; it is a demonstration of what AI-augmented development compresses, and what it does not.

Read it on effort, not calendar. The meaningful figure is roughly 150–160 focused hours against a ~735-hour industry baseline for comparable Level-3 e-learning — a 4.6–4.9× reduction. The eight-week calendar span overstates the working time: part-time early phases, a full week lost to illness, and an injury stretched the calendar without adding hours.

## Where the compression comes from

Three factors account for most of the difference. Approval and review cycles were eliminated — the designer and the decision-maker were the same person, so there were no phase gates. AI-accelerated code generation compressed the single largest workstream: a conventional front-end build of the platform would take 8–10 weeks; the AI-assisted build took two to three weeks of active development. And context continuity replaced the meetings and handoffs a distributed team needs — the process journal and system prompt carried all project context.

## What the team timeline buys

The organizational timeline is not simply slower. A team produces things a solo build cannot: multiple perspectives that challenge design decisions, distributed risk (the solo build lost a full week to illness with no one to absorb it), and the stakeholder alignment that phase-gate approvals create. The practical synthesis is a hybrid — use AI to compress implementation and documentation while preserving human-led analysis, design, and review — which points to roughly 14–18 weeks for an organization adopting AI tools without giving up multi-perspective review.
